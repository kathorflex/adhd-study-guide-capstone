Plate tectonics is the scientific theory that explains the movement of Earth’s outer shell, the lithosphere, which is divided into a set of large plates that float on the semi‑fluid asthenosphere beneath them.  There are three main types of plate boundaries: divergent boundaries where plates move apart (creating new crust, e.g., mid‑ocean ridges), convergent boundaries where plates collide (forming mountains, volcanoes, or deep‑sea trenches), and transform boundaries where plates slide past each other (producing earthquakes).  Most of Earth’s seismic and volcanic activity occurs at convergent and divergent boundaries.  The theory also explains continental drift, the formation of mountain ranges, and the spreading of the ocean floor.  Supporting evidence includes the fit of continental coastlines, matching rock formations across oceans, and magnetic striping on the sea floor.
