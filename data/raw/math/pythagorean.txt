In a right‑angled triangle, the square of the length of the hypotenuse (the side opposite the right angle) is equal to the sum of the squares of the lengths of the other two sides.  This relationship is expressed by the formula a² + b² = c², where c is the hypotenuse and a and b are the legs.  The theorem holds for any right‑angled triangle, regardless of the triangle’s size.  It can be proved in many ways – Euclid gave a geometric proof, while algebraic proofs use similar triangles or rearrangement of areas.  The Pythagorean theorem is the basis for distance calculations in two‑dimensional space and extends to three dimensions in the form a² + b² + d² = c².
