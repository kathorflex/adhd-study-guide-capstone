Supply and demand are the two fundamental forces that determine market prices.  The law of demand says that, all else being equal, a lower price leads to a higher quantity demanded.  Conversely, the law of supply says that a higher price encourages producers to supply more of a good.  When the supply curve and demand curve intersect, the market reaches equilibrium: the price at which the amount consumers want to buy equals the amount producers want to sell.  If the price is set above this equilibrium, a surplus results (more goods than buyers).  If it is set below equilibrium, a shortage occurs (more buyers than goods).  Shifts in either curve—caused by changes in technology, consumer preferences, or input costs—move the equilibrium price and quantity.
