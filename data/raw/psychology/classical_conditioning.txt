Classical conditioning was first described by Ivan Pavlov while studying the digestive secretions of dogs.  Pavlov noticed that the dogs began to salivate when they heard the laboratory assistant’s footsteps, even before the food was presented.  He identified three key components: an unconditioned stimulus (US) that naturally provokes a response, an unconditioned response (UR) that is automatic, a neutral stimulus (NS) that initially elicits no response, and a conditioned stimulus (CS) that, after repeated pairings with the US, evokes a conditioned response (CR).  After enough pairings of the bell (NS) with food (US), the bell alone caused the dogs to salivate—this salivation is the CR.  Classical conditioning explains how many simple associations are learned, from phobias to advertising jingles.
